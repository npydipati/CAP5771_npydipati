Milestone 2 - Data Wrangling

Date: 19 Feb 2026
Data Loading and Initial Cleaning
- What decisions did you make when loading and cleaning your data?
    - This week I loaded all 8 World Bank datasets into the notebook. Each CSV had 4 metadata rows at the top which needed to be skipped using skiprows=4. After loading, I found that
    each dataset had an "Unnamed" trailing column that pandas creates when the CSV has a trailing comma 
    — I dropped these immediately using str.contains("Unnamed").
    - The biggest decision this week was removing non-country entries. The World Bank datasets include aggregate rows like "World", "High income", "Sub-Saharan Africa", and about 40
    other regional and income-group aggregates. If left in, these would distort model training because they represent weighted averages of many countries, not actual
    observations. 
    - I also standardised the column names from "Country Name" and "Country Code" to "country" and "country_code" for consistency across all 8 datasets before any merging.

-  How did you transform and merge your datasets?
    - I reshaped all 8 datasets from wide format (one column per year) to long format (one row per country-year) using pd.melt(). Wide format is how the World Bank
    distributes the data but it is not suitable for modeling — you cannot run time-series operations like differencing or lagging on wide format data.
    - After reshaping, I filtered all datasets to 1990 onwards. Pre-1990 data has excessive missingness across most indicators, particularly electricity access which only begins
    in 1990 in the World Bank records. Keeping pre-1990 rows would add thousands of essentially empty rows with no analytical value.
    - For merging, I used electricity access as the base table and left-joined all other indicators on country and year. I chose left joins deliberately — electricity access
    is our target variable, so we keep all country-year combinations where it exists and accept NaNs for other indicators that may have slightly different country coverage.

One challenge: government effectiveness uses a different ISO code system than the other World Bank indicators, causing some countries to not match on merge. I handled
this by keeping the NaNs and addressing them in the cleaning stage rather than trying to force a join on mismatched keys.

- How did you handle missing values and why?
    - I addressed missing values across all 8 indicators. My strategy was per-country linear interpolation rather than global mean imputation or row dropping,
    for three reasons:

    1. Dropping rows would eliminate entire country-year combinations and create gaps in the time series, making lag and difference features unreliable.
    2. Global mean imputation ignores the fact that countries have very different development trajectories. Filling Uganda's missing GDP with the global mean GDP
    would introduce serious noise.
    3. Per-country linear interpolation estimates missing years from the surrounding known years of the same country, which is the most defensible approach for
    panel time-series data.
    - For government effectiveness specifically, which had ~70% missingness after interpolation, I filled remaining NaNs with the global median as a last resort.
    This column has poor coverage in the World Bank database for many developing countries and earlier years — it simply does not exist rather than being a data error.
    - I also dropped 2024 data entirely. World Bank 2024 figures are preliminary estimates released with a 1-2 year lag, not verified measurements, and 2024 is our forecast
    target year — including it in training would leak future information into the model.

- Final dataset after cleaning: 7,412 rows, 218 countries, 1990-2023.

Feature Engineering
- What features did you create and why?
    - This week I created 4 engineered features to support the modeling objective:

    1. risk_category — a categorical label derived from electricity_access: Severe (<50%), Moderate (50-89%), Minimal (90-100%). This is the classification
    target and directly represents energy poverty risk levels as defined in the project problem statement.
    2. elec_access_change — year-over-year difference in electricity access per country. This captures the rate of change rather than the absolute level, which is important
    for identifying countries that are rapidly improving vs stagnating.
    3. gdp_growth — year-over-year percentage change in GDP per capita. Economic growth rate is more informative than raw GDP level for predicting near-term changes in
    electricity access, since a rapidly growing economy is more likely to invest in infrastructure.
    4. urban_change — year-over-year change in urban population percentage. Urbanisation is strongly associated with electricity access improvements because urban grids are
    cheaper to extend than rural ones.

- One key decision: I did NOT include lagged_electricity or lagged_gdp as features. Initially I created them, but testing showed the regressor then learned almost entirely
from lagged_electricity (R²=0.9991 but 100% feature importance on one variable), essentially doing autoregression rather than learning economic drivers. Since our goal
is to forecast 2024-2027 from economic indicators, this was not acceptable.
