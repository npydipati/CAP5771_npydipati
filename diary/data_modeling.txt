Train/Val/Test Split
- How did you set up your modeling notebook and split your data?
    - This week I started the data modeling notebook. The first step was loading analysis_ready.csv and validating the schema — confirming all 15 columns loaded
    correctly, checking dtypes, null counts, and sample values before touching any model code.
    - I then applied the 80/10/10 temporal split:
        - Train: 1990–2017 (~80%) — 6,104 rows across 218 countries
        - Validate: 2018–2020 (~10%) — 654 rows
        - Test: 2021–2023 (~10%) — 654 rows
    - I chose temporal over random split because random splitting would leak future information into training — for example training on 2022 data and then predicting
    2019 is not realistic for a forecasting problem. Temporal split respects the real-world constraint that we only know the past when making predictions.
    - I verified zero overlap between the three sets — train years, validate years, and test years are completely disjoint sets with no shared values.
    - The class balance shifts across splits:
        - Severe drops from 22.7% (train) to 13.0% (val) to 9.6% (test)
        - Moderate: 17.3% (train) → 14.5% (val) → 17.1% (test)
        - Minimal rises from 60.0% (train) to 72.5% (val) to 73.2% (test)

    - Feature set: 10 socioeconomic features — gdp_per_capita, urban_population_percent, rural_population_percent, population_density, total_population,
    renewable_energy_percent, government_effectiveness, elec_access_change, gdp_growth, urban_change.

Note: lagged_electricity and lagged_gdp were intentionally excluded. Initial testing showed the regressor assigned nearly 100% feature importance to lagged_electricity,
essentially doing autoregression (predict this year = last year) rather than learning economic drivers. Since our goal is forecasting 2024-2027 from economic indicators
this was not acceptable.

Classification Models
- What classifiers did you build and which performed best?
    - I built three classifiers to predict risk_category (Severe / Moderate /Minimal) and evaluated them on the validation set (2018-2020).

    Results on validation set:

    1. RF Baseline (n_estimators=100, default params)
       CV Accuracy (5-fold): 0.9710 ± 0.0033
       Val Accuracy: 0.9235  ← BEST

    2. RF Improved (n_estimators=700, max_depth=14, min_samples_split=6,
       min_samples_leaf=2, max_features=0.6, class_weight='balanced')
       CV Accuracy (5-fold): 0.9641 ± 0.0049
       Val Accuracy: 0.9037  — worse than baseline

    3. XGBoost Baseline (n_estimators=300, max_depth=6, learning_rate=0.05,
       subsample=0.8, colsample_bytree=0.8)
       CV Accuracy (5-fold): 0.9766 ± 0.0067
       Val Accuracy: 0.9144

   4. XGBoost Improved (tuned hyperparameters)
      CV Accuracy (5-fold): 0.9744 ± 0.0064
      Val Accuracy: 0.9067

Surprising finding: RF Baseline outperformed all other classifiers on the validation set (0.9235) despite having the simplest configuration. The improved
RF and both XGBoost models actually scored lower on validation despite higher or comparable CV scores on training data. This suggests the more complex models
are slightly overfitting the training distribution which shifts in 2018–2020 as fewer countries are in Severe category.

The gap between CV accuracy (~0.97) and validation accuracy (~0.92) across all models reflects the temporal distribution shift — models trained on 1990–2017
see a different class balance than 2018–2020.

Best classifier: RF Baseline (0.9235 validation accuracy)
