MAJOR FINDINGS
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Finding 1 — Global Progress is Real but Slowing
Global electricity access grew from approximately 72% (1990) to 90% (2023) — an
18 percentage point gain over 33 years. However, the annual rate of gain peaked
around 2010–2015 and has visibly decelerated since. The last-mile problem is the
cause: connecting dense urban areas is cheap because infrastructure already exists
nearby; reaching dispersed rural populations in Sub-Saharan Africa requires
building entirely new grid infrastructure at very high cost per household.

Finding 2 — The Renewable Energy Paradox
The most surprising finding: renewable_energy_percent is the strongest predictor
in both models but the relationship is NEGATIVE. Countries with a high renewable
energy share tend to have lower electricity access. This seems contradictory until
you understand the World Bank's definition — their renewable metric includes
traditional biomass (wood, charcoal, animal dung), which is exactly the fuel used
by households with no grid connection. A country with 85% renewable energy may
simply be one where 85% of people burn wood because they have no electricity.
Policy implication: using renewable energy % as a headline metric for energy
progress is deeply misleading. An aid organization relying on it alone would
misclassify the most energy-poor countries as clean energy leaders.

Finding 3 — Governance Matters as Much as Wealth
government_effectiveness is the 5th most important feature for the regressor.
Countries with similar GDP can have dramatically different electricity access
outcomes depending on institutional quality. A wealthy but poorly governed country
fails to translate economic resources into infrastructure investment. This supports
the argument that governance reform is as important as direct infrastructure
spending for electrification progress.

Finding 4 — 27 Countries Stuck in Severe Risk by 2027
The model projects approximately 27 countries will remain below 50% electricity
access through 2027. Their shared profile: GDP per capita below $2,000, rural
population above 60%, renewable_energy_percent above 80% (traditional biomass),
government effectiveness score below -0.5. Almost all are in Sub-Saharan Africa.
Trend extrapolation is insufficient for these countries — they need targeted
structural intervention, not just continued monitoring.

Finding 5 — The Moderate Class is the Hardest to Predict (F1 = 0.59)
Countries in the 50–89% band are in active transition, and their trajectory is
highly sensitive to one-off events: a large infrastructure investment, a political
transition, or economic shock. The model's low confidence here is actually a useful
signal — Moderate-risk countries are high-leverage intervention targets where a
well-timed push can tip them into Minimal risk, and where human judgment is most
needed alongside the model output.

TRADEOFFS
━━━━━━━━━

Tradeoff 1 — Temporal Split vs. Random Split
A random 80/20 train/test split would have been simpler to implement and would
have produced higher apparent accuracy scores — the model could train on 2022
data and predict 2019, which is data leakage. A temporal split (1990–2017 train,
2018–2020 val, 2021–2023 test) is strictly correct for a forecasting problem but
produces lower, more honest scores. We chose the temporal split and accepted the
lower numbers. Every metric in this project reflects genuine out-of-sample
forecasting ability, not inflated in-sample fitting.

Tradeoff 2 — TimeSeriesSplit CV vs. StratifiedKFold CV
StratifiedKFold cross-validation returned ~0.97 accuracy for the classifier.
TimeSeriesSplit returned ~0.78 R² for the regressor. The gap — approximately 15
percentage points — represents the inflation from allowing future data to appear
in training folds. TimeSeriesSplit is slower and produces less impressive numbers,
but it is the only honest approach for temporal panel data. We chose honesty over
the more impressive headline number.

Tradeoff 3 — Keeping renewable_energy_percent vs. Removing It
After discovering the negative correlation, We considered removing this feature
entirely because it seemed paradoxical and We worried the model might learn a
spurious relationship. The tradeoff: keeping it gives the model its single most
predictive signal at the cost of requiring careful interpretation; removing it
produces a cleaner-seeming but less accurate model. We kept it because the
relationship is real and structurally grounded in how the World Bank defines
biomass, not noise. A high-predictive-power feature should not be dropped just
because the causal direction is counterintuitive.

Tradeoff 4 — Per-Country Interpolation vs. Global Mean Imputation
Global mean imputation is simple and fast. Per-country linear interpolation is
slower and more complex to implement. The tradeoff: global mean imputation would
fill Uganda's missing GDP with the world average GDP, introducing severe noise
for a developing country. Per-country interpolation estimates missing years from
the surrounding known years of the same country, which is far more defensible for
panel time-series data. We chose per-country interpolation and accepted the
additional implementation complexity.

Tradeoff 5 — Running Both Regression and Classification vs. One Model Type
Running both a regressor and a classifier doubled the modeling workload. The
tradeoff: a single classifier is simpler to explain and maintain; both together
produce more actionable output. A continuous percentage is useful for analysts;
a risk category is useful for policy audiences who need a clear priority signal.
We built both and accepted the added complexity because the combined output is more
useful than either alone.

Tradeoff 6 — RF Baseline vs. Tuned Models for Classification
The tuned RF classifier and both XGBoost models had higher cross-validation scores
than RF Baseline, but all performed worse on the validation set. The tradeoff:
choosing the tuned model would have optimized for CV score at the expense of
generalization; choosing the baseline gives up CV performance but generalizes
better when the class distribution shifts in 2018–2020. We selected the model that
performed best on the held-out validation set (RF Baseline), not the one with the
best cross-validation score. Principle: always choose based on held-out performance,
not training-time metrics.

SETBACKS AND HOW THEY WERE RESOLVED
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Setback 1 — Unnamed:69 Artifact Column in All Raw CSVs
During data exploration We discovered an "Unnamed:69" column in all 8 World Bank
datasets. This was created by pandas because the raw CSVs have a trailing comma
at the end of every row, causing pandas to read a 69th empty column that does not
exist in the data. Had this not been caught, it would have silently added a column
of NaN values to every merged dataset, corrupting null counts and model inputs.
Resolution: Added .loc[:, ~df.columns.str.contains("Unnamed")] as the first
cleaning step after every CSV load. Now the first operation after pd.read_csv().

Setback 2 — 2024 Preliminary Data Leaking Through the Melt Step
After reshaping all datasets from wide to long format using pd.melt(), a year <=
2023 filter was applied. Despite this, 2024 rows appeared in the final merged
dataset. The World Bank CSVs include a "2024" column of preliminary estimates that
survived the melt step because the filter ran before the merge, not after it.
Including 2024 in training would have leaked future information into the model.
Resolution: Added an explicit year <= 2023 filter after the final data quality
check, after all merges had completed. Lesson: filters applied before a merge do
not protect against columns introduced by the merge from a different source table.

Setback 3 — Lagged Features Caused the Regressor to Learn Autoregression
We initially included lagged_electricity (prior year's access) as a feature. The
regressor assigned ~100% feature importance to it and achieved R² = 0.9991 —
effectively learning "this year ≈ last year." This would be useless for 2024–2027
forecasting because we do not have future values of electricity access to feed
as lag inputs.
Resolution: Removed lagged_electricity and lagged_gdp entirely. Reran all 8
models. Performance dropped to R² = 0.91 on validation — an honest score based on
structural predictors rather than a trivially constructed lag.

Setback 4 — Government Effectiveness ISO Code Mismatch
The government effectiveness indicator uses a slightly different ISO country code
system than the other 7 indicators, causing ~30 countries to fail to match on the
merge key and producing NaN government_effectiveness values for those countries
even after interpolation.
Resolution: Rather than force an error-prone code translation, We accepted the NaNs
from the merge and applied global median fill as a last resort after per-country
interpolation. This is documented explicitly so reviewers understand it is a data
coverage limitation, not a pipeline bug.

Setback 5 — Tuned Models Underperformed the Baseline on Validation
After significant time tuning RF (n_estimators=700, max_depth=14,
class_weight='balanced') and XGBoost, all four tuned variants scored lower on the
validation set than the simple 100-tree RF Baseline. CV scores had suggested the
tuned models were better. This was a genuine surprise.
Resolution: Analysis revealed the class distribution shifts between training
(Severe = 22.7%) and validation (Severe = 13.0%). Tuned models — especially those
with class_weight='balanced' — had overfit to the training class balance. The
baseline, with no such assumption, generalized better to the shifted distribution.
Final selection: RF Baseline for classification. This was the correct result, not
a failure of the tuning process.

Setback 6 — Test R² Dropped More Than Expected (0.918 → 0.864)
The validation R² was 0.918. Test R² on 2021–2023 data was 0.864 — a 5.4 point
drop larger than the typical generalization gap. This required explanation before
reporting.
Resolution: Investigation showed 2021–2023 contains post-COVID patterns — supply
chain disruptions, economic contraction, and reporting delays — that differ from
the 1990–2020 training distribution. The drop is real but explained by external
circumstances, not model failure. Documented transparently in all outputs.
Lesson: always report both validation and test metrics; validation alone would
have overstated real-world performance.

REVISIONS MADE ACROSS THE PROJECT
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Revision 1 — Risk Category Thresholds (Quartile-Based → Fixed)
Originally We considered quartile-based thresholds that shift each year as global
access improves. We revised this to fixed thresholds (Severe <50%, Moderate 50–89%,
Minimal ≥90%) aligned with UN SDG7 benchmarks. Reason: fixed thresholds allow
meaningful year-to-year comparison. With quartile-based thresholds, a country
could move from Severe to Moderate between 2010 and 2015 purely because global
averages improved, even if nothing changed in that country.

Revision 2 — Data Scope (1960+ → 1990–2023)
The initial plan included all available World Bank data from 1960. After
exploration revealed pre-1990 data had >60% missingness for most indicators, I
revised the scope to 1990–2023. Pre-1990 rows would have added thousands of
nearly-empty observations that would have distorted imputation and made
year-over-year change features unreliable.

Revision 3 — Cross-Validation Method (StratifiedKFold → TimeSeriesSplit)
Initial modeling used StratifiedKFold. After noticing that CV accuracy (~0.97)
was much higher than validation accuracy (~0.92), We investigated and found temporal
data leakage within folds. We revised all CV to TimeSeriesSplit(n_splits=5),
reran all 8 models, and updated all reported scores. This reduced headline CV
numbers by ~15 percentage points but produced honest estimates.

Revision 4 — Feature Set (With Lags → Without Lags)
The initial feature set included lagged_electricity and lagged_gdp. After
discovering the autoregression problem, We revised the feature set to exclude both
lag variables and reran all 8 models from scratch. The final 10-feature set
reflects only structural indicators that would be genuinely available at forecast
time without needing future values of the target variable.

Revision 5 — Widget B Design (Single Year Static → Year Slider with Colour Encoding)
The original plan for Widget B was a static scatter plot of the most recent year.
After building Widget A and seeing the value of temporal exploration, We revised
Widget B to use a year slider (1990–2023) so users can watch the GDP vs
electricity access relationship evolve over time. The renewable energy colour
dimension was added specifically to communicate the paradox finding visually —
users can immediately see that countries with high renewable % (traditional
biomass, shown red) cluster in the low-access region of the chart.

REASONING BEHIND THE MOST CONSEQUENTIAL DECISIONS
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Decision — Why World Bank WDI as the Sole Data Source
We could have enriched the dataset with conflict data (ACLED), climate data
(ERA5), or infrastructure investment records (AidData). We chose WDI only for two
reasons: (1) reproducibility — anyone can download the exact same CSVs from the
World Bank portal and reproduce the entire pipeline without special data access;
(2) scope discipline — adding multiple sources would have expanded the project
beyond what was manageable while introducing alignment challenges across different
country codes, time ranges, and update cadences. A focused project with one
well-understood source produces more interpretable results.

Decision — Why SQLite in Addition to Flat CSVs
The raw data could have been stored only as flat CSVs. We added SQLite because:
(1) the normalized two-table structure (countries + indicators) eliminates
redundancy; (2) foreign key constraints prevent orphaned indicator rows;
(3) the long-format schema naturally supports temporal queries without reshaping.
It also provides a self-contained fallback for the Streamlit app when processed
CSVs are not present.

Decision — Why RF Baseline Beat Tuned Models and Why That Is the Correct Result
A 100-tree default Random Forest outperforming tuned 700-tree RF and XGBoost
initially appeared to indicate something was wrong. After analysis We concluded
it is the correct result. The training class distribution (Severe = 22.7%) differs
significantly from validation (Severe = 13.0%). Tuned models with class_weight=
'balanced' had baked in the assumption that class proportions are stable — an
assumption violated by temporal distribution shift. The baseline had no such
assumption and generalized better. General principle: on temporally shifting data,
simpler models with fewer distributional assumptions often outperform heavily
tuned ones.

WHAT WE WOULD DO DIFFERENTLY
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

1. Sub-national data instead of country averages
   Country-level averages hide massive within-country inequality — urban Nigeria
   and rural Nigeria have completely different situations folded into one number.
   Province-level data from the Demographic and Health Surveys (DHS) would have
   produced a far more actionable model for aid organizations.

2. SHAP values for individual country explanations
   Global feature importance tells you renewable_energy_percent matters across
   all 218 countries. SHAP would explain why Chad specifically is Severe. We had
   planned to add this in Milestone 2 but ran out of time. It would have been
   the most impactful addition to the policy communication layer.

3. Confidence intervals on the 2024–2027 forecasts
   Every forecast is a point estimate. With test MAE of 4.9 percentage points,
   a prediction of 58.3% could reasonably be 53–63%. Point predictions without
   uncertainty ranges create false precision. Bootstrap-based confidence intervals
   should have been part of the forecast output.

WHAT WENT WELL
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

1. Exploration caught every critical issue before modeling
   Unnamed:69, 2024 data leakage, and the renewable energy paradox were all
   discovered during EDA. Each was resolved before any model was trained.

2. Honest evaluation throughout
   TimeSeriesSplit CV, temporal split, reporting both validation and test metrics,
   and explaining the COVID-driven test performance drop — all reflect scientific
   honesty rather than optimizing for impressive headline numbers.

3. The full data science cycle is traceable end-to-end
   Raw CSV → SQLite → wrangling → analysis_ready.csv → modeling → output CSVs →
   visualization notebook → Streamlit app. Every stage has a dedicated notebook
   and diary entry.

4. The project delivers genuinely actionable output
   The 27-country Severe risk list, the renewable energy paradox, and the
   governance finding are results that an international development organization
   could act on directly.

FINAL THOUGHT
━━━━━━━━━━━━
The most important lesson from this project is that the decisions made before
modeling — how to define the problem, what time period to use, which features to
include, how to handle missing values, how to validate honestly — determine the
quality of the final result far more than which model is chosen. Random Forest vs.
XGBoost was a small decision. Temporal split vs. random split was a large one.
Excluding lagged electricity was a large one. Switching to TimeSeriesSplit was a
large one. The diary was valuable precisely because it forced articulation of why
those decisions were made at the time they were made, not rationalized after
seeing the results.
